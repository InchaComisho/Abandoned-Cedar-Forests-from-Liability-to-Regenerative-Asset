# From Abandoned Sugi Plantations to Regenerative Forest Assets

## Restoring Forests Through Fruit Trees, Wild Vegetables, Mushrooms, Humus, and Local Circular Value

## Overview

Many privately owned forests and abandoned sugi plantations in Japan are treated as financial burdens.

Owners may have to pay property taxes, deal with inheritance issues, manage unclear boundaries, worry about fallen trees, landslides, wildlife damage, and maintenance costs, while receiving little or no income from the land.

If timber prices are low and harvesting costs are high, the forest appears to have no economic value.

As a result, forests are left unmanaged.

Sugi plantations become neglected.
Undergrowth declines.
Humus weakens.
Microbial life is reduced.
Wildlife food sources disappear.

Eventually, animals such as bears, deer, and wild boars come closer to human settlements in search of food.

Humans then call this “wildlife damage.”

But is the forest itself really worthless?

This document argues that the problem is not the forest.

The problem is the human habit of evaluating forests only by timber price.

A forest is not merely a warehouse of wood.

A forest can be a regenerative asset that produces fruits, nuts, wild vegetables, mushrooms, humus, water, soil fertility, biodiversity, wildlife food sources, local food culture, and regional economic value.

This document proposes a shift from abandoned sugi plantations as liabilities to forests as regenerative circular assets.

---

## Core Conclusion

Forests are not liabilities by nature.

They become liabilities when humans can no longer see their value beyond timber.

Abandoned sugi plantations are not a failure of nature.

They are the result of a human perspective that reduced forests to wood production alone.

The necessary shift is not to abandon forests as unprofitable land.

The necessary shift is to redesign forests as regenerative assets that produce food, humus, mushrooms, water, biodiversity, soil life, wildlife habitat, and local economic value.

## Core Thesis

```text
Abandoned sugi plantation
  ↓
Evaluated only by timber price
  ↓
Low profitability
  ↓
Lack of management
  ↓
Loss of undergrowth, humus, food resources, and biodiversity
  ↓
Forest impoverishment
  ↓
Wildlife conflict, soil decline, and regional resource loss
```

This should be transformed into:

```text
Abandoned sugi plantation
  ↓
Thinning, diversification, and resource redesign
  ↓
Fruit trees, broadleaf trees, evergreen trees, wild vegetables, mushrooms
  ↓
Sawdust, wood chips, humus, mushroom substrate
  ↓
Soil regeneration, wildlife food recovery, and local resource creation
  ↓
Regenerative forest asset
```

## Forests Are Not Wood Warehouses

A mountain forest is not only a place for extracting timber.

A healthy forest can produce many forms of value.

* Fruits
* Nuts
* Wild vegetables
* Mushrooms
* Leaf litter
* Humus
* Soil fertility
* Insects
* Birds
* Small animals
* Food for bears, deer, wild boars, and other wildlife
* Water retention
* River nourishment
* Nutrient flow to farmland and oceans
* Local food culture
* Education
* Tourism
* Regional identity

In other words, forests produce food, soil, water, biodiversity, ecological stability, and regional resources.

However, modern forest management often evaluates forests mainly by whether their timber can be sold.

When forests are judged only by timber price, unmanaged forests appear worthless.

This is how forests become perceived as liabilities.

---

## Why Forest Ownership Becomes a Burden

Many forest owners face real burdens.

* Property taxes
* Inheritance procedures
* Unclear land boundaries
* Risk of fallen trees
* Landslide concerns
* Lack of labor
* Lack of management skills
* High harvesting and transport costs
* Low timber profitability
* Lack of local management systems
* No clear model for small-scale forest value creation

Under these conditions, it is understandable that owners lose motivation.

They may think:

“There is no profit.”
“It is difficult to sell.”
“Management costs money.”
“There is no one to take care of it.”
“Leaving it alone may be the only option.”

But stopping there is the problem.

The forest does not lack value.

The value extraction model is missing.

## Monoculture Sugi Plantations Are Weak as Forests and as Economies

After World War II, Japan planted large areas of sugi and hinoki for timber production.

At that time, this may have seemed necessary.

But a forest dominated by a single tree species is different from a naturally diverse forest.

Monoculture sugi plantations often have the following weaknesses:

* Few fruit-bearing trees
* Few edible nuts
* Limited wild vegetables
* Limited mushroom diversity
* Reduced variety of leaf litter
* Less undergrowth
* Fewer insects and small animals
* Fewer wildlife food sources
* Simpler soil circulation
* Excessive dependence on timber price
* Economic collapse when timber becomes unprofitable

A natural forest can produce many kinds of value.

A single-purpose plantation narrows that value.

If the only value is timber, the entire forest becomes economically fragile.

---

## Turning Forests Into Places for Fruits, Broadleaf Trees, Evergreens, Wild Vegetables, and Mushrooms

The solution begins by changing how forests are seen.

A forest should not be treated only as a timber field.

Possible directions include:

* Convert part of the forest into fruit-bearing areas
* Increase native broadleaf trees
* Include suitable evergreen species
* Plant locally adapted species
* Create areas where wild vegetables can grow
* Create conditions for mushrooms
* Restore undergrowth
* Return leaf litter to the soil
* Restore humus and microbial circulation
* Recover wildlife food sources inside the forest

If fruits and nuts increase in the mountains, they can become human food resources and local products.

At the same time, they also become food for wildlife inside the forest.

Wild vegetables and mushrooms can support local food culture, direct sales, restaurants, processing, tourism, and forest experience programs.

When broadleaf trees and evergreens increase, leaf litter becomes more diverse.

Diverse leaf litter supports richer soil.

Richer soil supports fungi and insects.

Fungi and insects support birds and small animals.

Over time, the entire food web begins to recover.

## Surplus Wood Is Not Waste

When sugi trees are thinned or harvested, not all wood can be used as construction timber.

But unusable timber is not waste.

It can be converted into many forms of value.

* Sawdust
* Wood chips
* Mushroom substrate
* Fungal beds
* Humus material
* Compost material
* Pathway material
* Mulch
* Moisture-retention material
* Soil improvement material
* Firewood
* Charcoal
* Wooden tableware
* Wooden crafts
* Signs
* Fences
* Benches
* Trail materials

If wood is ground into sawdust, microorganisms can decompose it more easily.

Sawdust can become material for humus and compost.

Wood blocks or chips can be used as mushroom substrate.

Wood chips can be used for paths, soil improvement, moisture retention, and weed suppression.

Better-shaped wood can become tableware, crafts, building material, firewood, charcoal, signs, fences, benches, or trail structures.

Wood does not lose value simply because it cannot be sold as timber.

Its value depends on how humans choose to use it.

## Sawdust, Wood Chips, Humus, and Mushroom Substrate as a Circular System

Forest restoration should not end with cutting trees.

The removed material should be returned to a useful cycle.

One possible cycle is:

```text
Thin sugi trees
  ↓
Use suitable parts for timber, tableware, or woodcraft
  ↓
Convert surplus wood into chips or sawdust
  ↓
Use chips and sawdust as mushroom substrate
  ↓
Compost used substrate into humus
  ↓
Return humus to forests and fields
  ↓
Soil becomes richer
  ↓
Plants grow
  ↓
Fungi and insects increase
  ↓
Wildlife food sources recover
  ↓
Forest circulation is restored
```

In this model, the forest does not produce waste.

It produces circulation.

In nature, there is no true waste.

One life uses what another life leaves behind.

Organic matter is decomposed, transformed, and passed into the next cycle of life.

The human role should not be to stop that circulation.

The human role should be to help broken circulation restart.

## Forest Owners Need More Options Than “Cut or Abandon”

Forest ownership is often reduced to extreme choices:

* Cut the trees
* Leave the forest alone
* Sell the land
* Give up

But there are more options.

The entire forest does not need to be changed at once.

Possible small-scale steps include:

* Convert a small area into fruit-bearing trees
* Restore wild vegetables along accessible paths
* Plant nut or fruit trees in sunny areas
* Prioritize water and humus circulation in valleys
* Keep steep slopes as protection forests
* Convert thinned wood into chips or mushroom substrate
* Connect with local restaurants and farm shops
* Create forest experience programs
* Use the area for environmental education
* Develop small-scale local branding

It becomes difficult when the entire mountain is treated as one product.

If the forest is divided into multiple functions, value can be created gradually.

---

## Enriching Forests Can Also Reduce Wildlife Conflict

Bears, deer, wild boars, and other animals often approach human settlements because food inside the mountain has become insufficient.

If forests provide enough food, animals have less reason to risk entering human settlements.

Human voices, cars, dogs, roads, traps, and artificial structures are usually warning signs for wildlife.

If animals still approach, it often means that the mountain no longer provides enough conditions for survival.

When fruits, nuts, undergrowth, insects, small animals, humus, and water return to the forest, wildlife can live more easily inside the mountain.

Of course, short-term measures are also necessary.

Human settlements must manage garbage, abandoned fruits, crop residues, and food odors.

But these are short-term measures.

The root solution is to restore forests so that animals can survive within their natural habitat.

Forest regeneration is also wildlife conflict prevention.

It restores the boundary between human settlements and wildlife habitat.

## Examples of Forest Asset Conversion

Forest value is not limited to timber price.

### 1. Fruits and Nuts

Chestnuts, persimmons, plums, yuzu, walnuts, yamamomo, acorns, and other locally suitable fruit-bearing or nut-bearing trees can provide food for humans and wildlife.

They can also support processing, direct sales, local cuisine, and regional branding.

### 2. Wild Vegetables

Tara-no-me, fuki, warabi, zenmai, kogomi, udo, and other local wild vegetables can become food resources, restaurant ingredients, and tourism experiences.

### 3. Mushrooms

Logs, branches, chips, and sawdust can be used as mushroom substrate or fungal beds.

This makes use of the forest’s humidity and wood resources.

### 4. Humus and Compost

Leaf litter, branches, sawdust, and used mushroom substrate can be composted or turned into humus.

Returning humus to forests and fields supports soil regeneration.

### 5. Wood Chips

Wood chips can be used for pathways, mulch, soil improvement, livestock bedding, compost material, moisture retention, and weed suppression.

### 6. Wooden Products and Tableware

Wood that is not suitable for construction may still be used for bowls, plates, chopsticks, utensils, signs, crafts, and small products.

### 7. Education, Tourism, and Experience Programs

Forests can support wild vegetable gathering, mushroom cultivation, humus making, forest restoration workshops, nature observation, and children’s environmental education.

With creativity, forests can produce layered value.

## The Problem Is Not Lack of Resources, but Lack of Imagination

Japan has mountains.
It has trees.
It has leaf litter.
It has water.
It has fungi.
It has wild vegetables.
It has mushrooms.
It has regional food cultures.

The resources exist.

The problem is that these resources are not connected.

If wood cannot be sold, the forest is abandoned.
If management costs money, the forest is ignored.
If animals appear, they are killed.
If the mountain degrades, people wait for government intervention.

This does not solve the problem.

What is needed is to redesign forests as circulation systems.

Timber, fruits, wild vegetables, mushrooms, humus, microorganisms, water, wildlife, local food culture, and regional economy should not be treated separately.

They should be connected as one regenerative system.

When this happens, abandoned forests can become regenerative assets.

## What Governments, Owners, and Communities Should See

### Governments

Governments should not look only at wildlife damage control.

They should also look at mountain food resources, forest diversity, soil regeneration, and habitat restoration.

Capture, fences, and warnings may reduce immediate risk.

But if the mountain remains poor, another animal will come down again.

### Forest Owners

Forest owners should not evaluate forests only by timber price.

They should also see fruits, wild vegetables, mushrooms, humus, education, experience programs, and local resources.

The entire forest does not need to be transformed at once.

Regenerative asset conversion can begin from one small area.

### Local Communities

Local communities should not see forests only as troublesome land.

Forests can be water sources, disaster prevention systems, food resources, ecological infrastructure, education fields, tourism assets, and cultural foundations.

If forests are connected to local restaurants, farm shops, schools, tourism, agriculture, and environmental activities, their value expands.

## Implementation Model

### Stage 1: Site Assessment

* Confirm ownership boundaries
* Check slope, water flow, roads, and sunlight
* Identify current tree species
* Identify fallen or dangerous trees
* Observe animal paths
* Identify existing wild vegetables, mushrooms, fruits, and usable resources

### Stage 2: Small-Scale Conversion

* Start from accessible paths
* Thin dangerous or overcrowded trees
* Convert wood into chips or sawdust
* Plant a small number of fruit-bearing and broadleaf trees
* Preserve areas where wild vegetables grow
* Test mushroom substrate production
* Create humus from leaves, branches, and sawdust

### Stage 3: Circular Use

* Separate harvested material into timber, crafts, chips, and substrate
* Compost used mushroom substrate
* Return humus to the forest
* Turn fruits, wild vegetables, and mushrooms into local resources
* Restore wildlife food sources inside the mountain
* Reduce attractants near human settlements

### Stage 4: Regional Asset Development

* Connect with farm shops
* Connect with restaurants
* Connect with schools
* Create forest experience programs
* Create forest restoration tours
* Develop local branding
* Link forest regeneration with local food culture

## Conclusion: Forests Are Not Liabilities. Human Abandonment of Forest Value Makes Them Liabilities.

Abandoned forests and sugi plantations are indeed burdens under the current system.

Property taxes, management costs, inheritance, fallen trees, disasters, and wildlife conflict can make ownership feel like a liability.

But the forest itself is not without value.

The value is invisible only because humans are looking at forests through timber price alone.

Forests contain fruits.
They contain nuts.
They contain wild vegetables.
They contain mushrooms.
They contain leaf litter.
They contain humus.
They contain water.
They contain microorganisms.
They contain wildlife food sources.
They contain local resources.

Even after sugi trees are cut, they can still be used.

They can become tableware.
They can become woodcraft.
They can become chips.
They can become sawdust.
They can become mushroom substrate.
They can become humus.
They can become compost.
They can return to the forest.

It depends on imagination and design.

Forests are not liabilities by nature.

They become liabilities when humans refuse to see their full value.

Abandoned forests are not failures of nature.

They are the result of a civilization that forgot how to see forests as living circulation systems.

The task now is not to give up on unprofitable forests.

The task is to transform them into regenerative assets that support fruits, wild vegetables, mushrooms, humus, water, biodiversity, wildlife habitat, local food culture, and regional economy.

Forest regeneration is not only for animals.

It is also for humans.

If mountains become rich again, animals can live in the mountains.
Humans can receive the blessings of the mountains.
Soil can return.
Water can return.
Local communities can return.

Whether forests remain liabilities or become regenerative assets is not a problem of nature.

It is a problem of human imagination.
